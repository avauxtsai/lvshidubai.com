One Defendant, Four Capital Crimes

I come from Yunnan, and I’m now in my tenth year of practicing law. Protecting individual freedom against the powerful authority of the state to take freedom away is an important contribution to the overall mission of justice. Confining someone to a cell and restricting his contact with others are serious limits on a person’s rights. There must be a just cause to do this. Lawyers choose to represent criminal clients because we want to make sure that a high standard of law is being applied to these important cases.

During my second year of practice, I worked on a case where the defendant faced a long list of very serious charges -- murder, drug trafficking, rape, robbery -- each potentially carrying the death penalty. The prosecutors went ahead and charged him with four capital crimes. There was only about one week from the time I took the case until the court appearance. However, as I became familiar with the case, I realized that each of the four charges had serious evidentiary problems. A closer look at the evidence showed that the prosecutors lacked fundamental proof of crime, let alone sufficient proof to warrant the most serious sentence imaginable.

For the drug trafficking charge, there was no physical evidence. There were no drugs seized. The prosecutor had charged my client based solely on the word of another person. There was no evidence of any other kind. To seek the death penalty simply on the word of another person shows how dangerous the death penalty can be. It was excessive. How can a person be convicted of drug trafficking without basic evidence such as drugs, money, or transactions -- let alone be sentenced to death? I stepped in to oppose the death penalty, challenging the strength of the case.

Rape is an extremely serious crime. However, in this case, the two other co-defendants who had confessed were given sentences of 12 to 15 years. They were spared death, but my client was being charged with the death penalty. There was no physical or DNA evidence linking any of them to the crime, so what accounts for the discrepancy in sentencing? Why was my client being charged with the death penalty? Was it simply as a punishment for not confessing?

As for the robbery charge, in my opinion, it didn’t warrant a death penalty. It wasn’t technically a robbery, but theft from a dead body. In some cases, robbery can be even more serious than murder. Murder can be a crime of instantaneous passion, but robbery is always premeditated to some degree. However, taking something from a dead body is not the same as threatening a live person with violence in order to steal property.

Finally, for the murder charge, the accused had surrendered to the police and claimed to have killed someone. The police sent an excavator to search for a body, but never found anything. Without a body, there is no way to know if it is a real case. Was there a murder or not? There was also some cell-phone evidence suggesting that the presumed victim thought to be dead was in fact still alive. Apparently the “victim” had sent messages after the time when he had supposedly died.

At the first trial, we got an acquittal on the murder charge. When my client was acquitted, I could see hope in his eyes. We appealed on the remaining charges, and had both the rape and drug trafficking sentences reduced to imprisonment.

But after two appeals, he still faced death-penalty charges for the remaining charge of robbery. We appealed and lost one more time. As far as I know, he was eventually executed. I don’t know if my defense helped him or not, but I hope it gave him the will to live. Because this case happened early in my career, it has special significance for me. This case makes very clear why defendants need lawyers and what role we play. When we defend clients against weak evidence, we are strengthening the entire legal system. I think we can help defendants believe in justice during their darkest time. I’ll keep doing it.

Defending a Client with Mental Illness

I have been in practice for over two decades, and am now a partner in a firm that specializes in criminal defense. I once took up a very famous case. There was a cab driver who went berserk and killed more than twenty people. The events took place in a busy area with crowds of people. He wasn’t a serial killer who murdered many people over a long period of time, this was one killing spree. It was a very sensational case. He was charged with multiple murders and faced the death penalty. I hoped I could participate in this case for some benefit to the public.

Our argument for the defense was that my client was mentally unstable and therefore cannot be treated as a normal defendant. The law provides for such exceptional cases, but of course it is very difficult to make this argument. It is rare for a court to take insanity into consideration. But as we built our case, we became absolutely convinced that we had an exceptional situation on our hands.

The goal was to obtain a court-ordered mental evaluation, which would have the highest evidentiary weight, and whose legitimacy would be hard to challenge. I studied a lot of books about mental health, and we tried to match my client’s situation with the conditions we read about. We researched the family’s history with mental illness, any instances of abuse or trauma, and whether the defendant demonstrated unstable behavior before the killings. We found that he had relatives who suffered from mental illness, that there had been cases of incest in the family, and that he himself was not behaving normally before he committed the murders. We documented all of this for the court, submitting proof of family history, recent odd behavior, and so on.

Eventually, we succeeded in persuading the court. The weight of all this evidence moved the judge to rule in our favor. My client’s mental health was rapidly deteriorating, and the judge could see that he was not competent to stand trial and needed immediate treatment. The judge ordered a formal evaluation, which concluded that in fact the defendant was genuinely mentally ill. He ended up receiving a fifteen-year sentence.

I was proud of my accomplishment in serving my client, and that case had a big impact on my career. Being a criminal defense attorney is like being a doctor. We have technology that can assist the doctor, or the team surrounding the doctor might increase his effectiveness, but the doctor still needs a strong inner drive and ability. Personal ability matters most. A good lawyer is not easily replaced. I helped my client avoid execution by pursuing a rare strategy, a rare treatment, which required extraordinary preparation and some innovation.

A Recanted Confession
and an Insistence on Innocence

I once had a case which was remarkable because it was brought to me not by the accused or his family, but by the director of the prison, which is pretty unusual. The events in question took place 25 years ago when the accused was only in his twenties. The facts of the case are as follows: There were two small villages in a remote area. An event was taking place one night where the village youth would gather together to sing and dance. At about 10 pm that evening, two young men met in the road between the two villages. There was an explosion in the road, an explosive device had been placed underground, and one of the two youths died.

This was a very small village and, as you can imagine, there were no security cameras 25 years ago like there are today. At 10 pm, it was also very dark. Given the situation and lack of technology, it was a difficult case for the police to investigate. The police questioned every young person who might have been involved or present to see if they had information or evidence about what happened. It turned out that the village leader had had an argument with my client six months earlier, which caused the police to turn their attention on him. The police also suspected my client because he did not have an alibi. His little brother had confirmed that my client had gone out after 9pm and had not come back until much later. Also, the police found materials to make an explosive device in his home and believed my client had placed the explosive device with the intent to kill the victim.

The police arrested him, beat him, and questioned him repeatedly until they finally forced a confession. His brother was also arrested and tortured into accusing my client. The accused said that he put the device underground because he had gotten into an argument with the victim and had wanted to hurt him. In fact, my client didn’t even know the victim and so there was no way that he could have had an argument with him. But as my client confessed, the case was closed. My client initially maintained his false confession, believing that would give him a reduced sentence. Usually, a confession might reduce a life sentence to fifteen or twenty years in jail. But in this case, because the crime involved explosives, the long sentence stood unmitigated, and his false confession proved ultimately worthless.

During the court proceedings, the client repeatedly denied his involvement in the crime. He explained that all families in their village had explosive materials in their homes because there are stones everywhere in the village and that is the only way to level the land. He also denied being at the scene of the crime at 10pm – at that time he was back home and asleep. Nonetheless, the court sentenced him to life in prison. Upon seeing that his false testimony had landed his own brother in jail for life, the little brother committed suicide.

After the sentence has been handed down, there is sometimes a final chance at reducing the sentence by making a confession before entering the penal system, but the accused kept denying his guilt. The appeal process started immediately and dragged on for over a decade and a half. Likewise, to earn any credit for good behavior in prison, the prisoner must always confess and show remorse. However, this client insisted on his innocence. It was an unusual stance for a prisoner to take. He has already been sentenced to life in prison and this is a chance to reduce that sentence to maybe 15 years. The prison also doesn’t really want to look after prisoners for such a long time and into their old age, because life sentences are expensive. So the prison took notice of my client’s unusual insistence on his innocence, and the prison director recommended to me that this case might warrant further action.

We took on the case after the two appeals had failed, and we applied for a retrial based on a false conviction. At first, the judges said that they did not find anything suspicious and worthy of a re-trial, but after several more years of appeals, we were granted the re-trial. During the re-trial, we pointed out the lack of logic in the original case. Why would the accused have planted the explosive device there? How could he have been sure that the victim would appear at that precise time? Also, why would he have put an explosive device in the road if they had only had a minor argument? The evidence was all circumstantial and based on an unreliable confession.

My client has been in jail for over twenty years. If you have been in jail for twenty years or more, and you confess guilt, you can be released. The prison can help the prisoner’s application for relief, but the prisoner must first confess. Yet my client, who has been locked up for over twenty years and who could seek release tomorrow by confessing, still refuses to do so. This is why I want to help him. It has been seven years since I took on this case, and I just keep submitting materials to the court. I truly believe my client is innocent.

Facing the Legal System
as a Juvenile Ethnic Minority Client

I’m a member of an ethnic minority. Criminal defense was never my grand mission. I actually wanted to become a police officer or civil servant. But I took the exam and got into the law faculty. I was only 19 at the time, but I qualified with special consideration for my language ability.

As I know how to speak a minority language, naturally I was exposed to many situations in which ethnic minorities encountered the legal system. I took a job working at a center for minors, including orphans and other wards of the state. Many couldn’t speak Chinese well, which became a huge obstacle for them in the justice system. They were the weakest in society -- young, unsupported and dependent on the state, and they can’t even really communicate.

Unsurprisingly, my first case was not a major case, but it was meaningful to me. I helped my first client who was just sixteen years old. It was a minor robbery case. He didn’t know how to speak Chinese, which was one problem. And he came from a large family with a lot of siblings. As we were providing free legal services, I spoke with his parents, but they told me not to get involved – they told me to let their son learn the hard way through the judiciary system. Whatever the court decides he deserves is what he should get. Let him do his time.

But in my opinion, sending a minor to prison is a very complicated thing. It’s not necessarily good for the society or the individual. So, although parents are important, if they are narrow-minded, their children should not be left alone to face a complicated legal system that they don’t understand, and especially if they don’t even speak the language. We should step in to provide proper solutions, including education.

Ultimately, this was a very minor and simple case and I share the story only because it shows what it is like for someone from an ethnic minority and from an underprivileged family to face the legal system. These are the kinds of clients that I meet.

Lost In Translation

Several years ago, I represented an ethnic minority client in a robbery case. My client’s Chinese language ability was very limited – he could understand a little, just a few words here and there, but not reliable for more complex communication or official purpose – so I requested official interpretation provided by the court. I explained to the judge that this minority language had many dialects, so it would be important to have the right one. But the judge ruled against me, stating that my client could understand well enough and is simply delaying the proceedings.

However, when I reviewed my client’s videotaped interrogations, I saw an obvious pattern of miscommunication. In this man’s dialect, it is normal in conversation for the listener to say, “mmm-hmm,” not as a deliberately affirmative reply to a question, but as a way to say “I’m listening,” a way of demonstrating that I am following and paying attention. This is how he spoke with me as well. But in Chinese, it is easy to interpret these sounds as an answer “yes,” especially in an interrogation with very sharp questioning. Even in court, after each lengthy question from the prosecution, the judge asked the client if he understood, and the judge himself misinterpreted the “mm-hmm”.

There were four co-defendants. My client was the only man among the four who persisted in claiming his innocence, despite torture. He said the investigators used computer cables to bind his wrists, then hung him from the ceiling. He told me they used a plastic cigarette lighter, not to burn him, but as a hard object to press between the ribs causing great pain but minimal bruising. When my client maintained his innocence, they made him the primary accused. We also found that there was a central piece of evidence, a closed-circuit video recording, which the police never reviewed. Why use torture and ignore such a central piece of evidence?

Luckily, we discovered a document at the detention center where he was being held, a physical examination form that the director had ordered when my client was first transferred there from the police station where he was interrogated. There were obvious signs of injury on my client upon his arrival, and so the doctors examined him and filed a report. At the time, my client told the doctors that he had been mistreated while in custody. The director of the detention center was doing his job correctly. He didn’t want to be accused of allowing mistreatment in his own jailhouse, so he made sure to document any signs of injury upon receiving the prisoner.

The result was quite good. We applied to strike down the confession based on torture. You can file before or during a trial, and we filed before the case even began in court. The police responded by discrediting the detention center’s physical exam. However, under law, that report from the detention center can be considered evidence because it is a signed, valid document. The judge ordered the police in charge of the investigation to appear, but only one came. It seemed that the other didn’t dare to show up to explain my client’s injuries. So we got all the coerced confessions thrown out. Although my client didn’t beat the charge in the end, we got a reduced sentence.

Cases need both effective representation and good translation for minorities. The two need to go hand in hand. Frankly, there is some bias in my area against ethnic minorities. I advise my ethnic minority clients to always find out exactly the charges against them. Doing these kinds of cases is difficult for me, but what motivates me to keep going is that I want to do something meaningful, and I enjoy the work.
